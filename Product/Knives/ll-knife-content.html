Your guide to choosing the perfect knife

First, consider which type of knife

Santoku knife

Santoku knive originated in Japan and are designed for slicing, dicing and mincing. Most santoku knives have flat divots on the sides that help keep food from sticking to the blade.

Chef's knife

A chef's knive is also known as a cook's knife. It is multi-purpose and can be used for mincing, slicing, chopping veggies, slicing meat and disjointing large cuts

Bread knife

The serrated blades are able to cut through soft bread without crushing it. They can also be used for slicing tough skinned fruits such as tomatoes.

Cleaver

A cleaver is a largely used at home as a butcher's knife for hacking through bone. The knife's broad blade can be used for crushing garlic and peppercorns.

Paring knife

A paring knife is a great all-purpose knife, similar to a chef's knife only smaller. It is perfect for peeling, mincing an onion and herbs or segmenting fruit.

Filleting knife

A filleting knife's very flexible blades are used to prepare and filet fish allowing them to move more easily along the backbone and under the skin of a fish.

Anatomy of a knife

Familiarising yourself with the elements of a knife will assist you in finding the right one for you.

What to look for in a knife

1. Type of knife

Before purchasing knives, consider the type of knife you will need in your kitchen and what you'll be using it for. Chopping hard veggies? Carving thin slices of meat? Fine mincing and dicing?

2. The feel

When buying knives you should ideally hold each type in your hand. If you're going to have it in your kitchen for many years of use, the knife should feel comfortable and sit well in your hand.

3. The weight

It's important to feel the weight of a knife when making your selection. A lighter knife is good for speed and precision, whereas a heavier knife is better for chopping hard veggies and nuts.

4. Amount of steel

Look at how much steel is in the knife. Look for any signs of joining or welding. Good knives are made from a single piece of steel, hand forged, while cheap knives tend to be thin with handles covered in thick plastic.

5. Balance

Good knives should be well balanced. The easiest way to test this is to place your finger horizontally at the heel of the knife. A good knife should balance and not fall off. Balanced knives are more comfortable to use.

6. Composition

Consider what the knife is made from. Fully forged blades are great because the forging makes the metal stronger. Ceramic blades often stay sharp longer but aren't meant to be used on hard or frozen foods.
